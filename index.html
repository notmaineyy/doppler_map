Understanding the Doppler Visibility Map
Step 1 — How pulsed radar measures velocity
A pulsed radar fires short bursts of radio waves and listens for echoes. When a target is moving, the returning echo has a slightly different frequency from what was transmitted — this shift is called the Doppler shift. A target moving towards the radar returns a higher frequency; moving away returns a lower one. By measuring this shift, the radar calculates the target's radial velocity (speed directly towards or away from the radar).
Doppler shift = 2 × v × f / c
where v = target velocity, f = radar frequency, c = speed of light
Step 2 — The sampling problem (why blind speeds exist)
A pulsed radar doesn't transmit continuously — it fires pulses at a rate called the PRF (Pulse Repetition Frequency), e.g. 1000 pulses per second. This means it samples the Doppler shift 1000 times per second. Just like recording audio at too low a sample rate causes aliasing, sampling the Doppler at a finite PRF causes certain target velocities to become indistinguishable from a stationary target. These are the blind speeds.
v_blind = n × λ × PRF / 2
where n = 0, ±1, ±2, … and λ = radar wavelength
At these exact velocities, the phase shift between consecutive pulses is a perfect multiple of 360° — the radar sees no change pulse-to-pulse and concludes the target is stationary (or non-existent).
Step 3 — Unambiguous velocity (v_ua)
The distance between consecutive blind speeds is called the unambiguous velocity interval (v_ua). Inside this window, every velocity produces a unique Doppler reading — the radar can correctly measure it.
v_ua = λ × PRF / 2
For example, an X-band radar (λ = 3 cm) at PRF = 1000 Hz gives:
v_ua = 0.03 × 1000 / 2 = 15 m/s
This means blind speeds occur every 15 m/s — quite limiting for fast targets. The v_ua value shown under each slider in this tool updates live as you drag, so you can see this relationship directly.
Step 4 — What you see on each row
Each horizontal row in the chart is one PRF. Reading across the row left to right:

— Coloured band = target at that velocity IS detectable.
— Red band = target at that velocity is in a blind zone — it looks stationary and the radar cannot detect it.

Notice how the red bands are evenly spaced within each row. The spacing equals v_ua for that PRF. A higher PRF pushes those red bands further apart (wider v_ua), so you see fewer but more widely spaced blind zones.
Step 5 — Why use multiple PRFs (staggering)
No single PRF is perfect — blind speeds are unavoidable. The solution is to transmit several different PRFs in sequence. Each PRF has its blind speeds at different velocities, because v_ua = λ × PRF / 2 changes with PRF. The Combined row at the bottom shows which velocities a target is detectable at by all PRFs simultaneously — the blind gaps collapse dramatically.

Try this: set just 1 PRF and observe how much of the axis is red. Then increase to 3 or 6 PRFs and watch the Combined row fill in.
Step 6 — Choosing good PRF ratios
The best stagger performance comes from PRFs whose ratios are simple integers with a large least-common-multiple. For example:
4:5:6 → e.g. 1000, 1250, 1500 Hz
5:6:7 → e.g. 1000, 1200, 1400 Hz
These ratios mean the first velocity where all PRFs are simultaneously blind is pushed very high — far beyond the velocity range of interest. Conversely, if you set two PRFs very close together (e.g. 1000 and 1010 Hz), their blind speeds nearly coincide and the Combined row becomes mostly red. Try it with the sliders.
Step 7 — Blind zone width
A real radar doesn't just lose targets at a single exact velocity — it loses them over a small band around each blind speed. This is because the clutter rejection filter (which suppresses stationary ground returns) has a finite roll-off. The blind zone width slider in this tool simulates that width as a percentage of v_ua. Increasing it models a more aggressive filter, widening the red bands and reducing coverage. Typical real values are 3–8%.
Step 8 — Coverage % explained
The percentage in brackets on each row label (e.g. "PRF1 1000 Hz (95%)") tells you what fraction of the total velocity axis is not in a blind zone for that PRF. A single PRF with 5% blind zone width gives ~95% individual coverage, because the red bands take up 5% of each v_ua interval. The Combined coverage is lower, because blind zones from different PRFs sometimes overlap. The goal of PRF design is to choose values that maximise combined coverage.
