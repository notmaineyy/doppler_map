understanding the doppler visibility map 8 sections
▼
Step 1 — How pulsed radar measures velocity
A pulsed radar fires short bursts of radio waves and listens for echoes. When a target is moving, the returning echo has a slightly different frequency from what was transmitted — this shift is called the Doppler shift. By measuring this shift, the radar calculates the target's radial velocity.
Doppler shift = 2 × v × f / c
where v = target velocity, f = radar frequency, c = speed of light
Step 2 — The sampling problem (why blind speeds exist)
A pulsed radar samples the Doppler shift at a rate called the PRF. Just like audio aliasing, certain target velocities become indistinguishable from a stationary target — these are the blind speeds.
v_blind = n × λ × PRF / 2
where n = 0, ±1, ±2, …
Step 3 — Unambiguous velocity (v_ua)
The distance between consecutive blind speeds is the unambiguous velocity interval (v_ua).
v_ua = λ × PRF / 2
For X-band (λ = 3 cm) at PRF = 1000 Hz:
v_ua = 0.03 × 1000 / 2 = 15 m/s
The v_ua value shown under each slider updates live.
Step 4 — What you see on each row
Each horizontal row is one PRF. Reading left to right:

— Coloured band = target IS detectable.
— Red band = blind zone — radar cannot detect the target.

A higher PRF pushes red bands further apart (wider v_ua).
Step 5 — Why use multiple PRFs (staggering)
Each PRF has its blind speeds at different velocities. The Combined row shows which velocities are detectable by all PRFs simultaneously. Try: set 1 PRF, observe red coverage, then increase to 6 and watch the Combined row fill in.
Step 6 — Choosing good PRF ratios
Best performance comes from PRFs with simple integer ratios and large LCM:
4:5:6 → e.g. 1000, 1250, 1500 Hz
5:6:7 → e.g. 1000, 1200, 1400 Hz
Two very close PRFs (e.g. 1000 and 1010 Hz) nearly coincide in blind speeds, making the Combined row mostly red.
Step 7 — Blind zone width
A real radar loses targets over a small band around each blind speed, not at a single exact velocity. The blind zone width slider simulates this as a percentage of v_ua — modelling the roll-off of the clutter rejection filter. Typical real values are 3–8%.
Step 8 — Coverage % explained
The percentage on each row label tells you what fraction of the velocity axis is not in a blind zone. A single PRF at 5% blind zone width gives ~95% individual coverage. Combined coverage is lower because blind zones from different PRFs can overlap. The goal is to maximise this.
