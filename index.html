The radar carrier frequency. This determines the wavelength: λ = c / f. Higher frequency = shorter wavelength = smaller unambiguous velocity per PRF. X-band (9.5 GHz) is typical for weather and airborne radars.

? PRF Settings
PRF = Pulse Repetition Frequency — how many pulses the radar fires per second (Hz). A higher PRF means the radar "samples" the Doppler shift more often, which raises the unambiguous velocity v_ua = λ × PRF / 2. But higher PRFs also reduce the maximum unambiguous range. Staggering multiple PRFs trades off these constraints.

How to read the Doppler Visibility Map
What you're looking at
Each horizontal row represents one PRF. The row is coloured where a target at that velocity can be detected, and red where it cannot (a "blind speed"). The bottom row (Combined) shows which velocities are simultaneously detectable by all PRFs — this is what the radar actually sees.
Why blind speeds exist
A pulsed radar samples the received signal at the PRF rate. Just like audio aliasing, a target whose Doppler-shift frequency equals a multiple of the PRF "folds back" to zero — it looks stationary to the radar. These are the blind speeds:
vblind = n × λ × PRF / 2
where n = 0, ±1, ±2, …
Unambiguous velocity (v_ua)
Between consecutive blind speeds lies the unambiguous velocity interval. Any target in this window produces a unique, correctly measured Doppler reading. The interval width is:
v_ua = λ × PRF / 2
Higher PRF or longer wavelength = wider interval. The coverage % shown in the strip tells you what fraction of the velocity axis falls inside visible zones.
Why stagger multiple PRFs
A single PRF has blind speeds at regular intervals. By using several PRFs with different values, each PRF's blind speeds fall at different velocities. The Combined row shows only velocities that are visible to every PRF at once — the blind gaps are much narrower than for any single PRF, greatly improving detection probability across all speeds.
Freq Domain tab
Shows each PRF's harmonic lines — the exact positions of the blind speeds on the velocity axis. Solid lines = the fundamental (first blind speed). Dotted lines = harmonics. Good for checking whether any two PRFs share a blind speed at the same velocity.
Coverage % (top strip)
The percentage of the velocity axis that is visible (not blind) for each PRF individually, and for the combined set. A single PRF with 5% blind zone width gives ~95% individual coverage, but the combined coverage drops as blind zones occasionally overlap. The goal of PRF design is to maximise combined coverage.
Choosing good PRF ratios
Simple integer ratios like 4:5, 4:5:6, or 5:6:7 give the best stagger performance because their least-common-multiple is large — meaning the first velocity where all PRFs are simultaneously blind is pushed very high. Prime-number ratios (e.g. 5:7:11) give even larger first combined blind speeds.
Blind zone width slider
Real clutter filters aren't perfect — they suppress a small band of velocities around each blind speed, not just a single point. This slider simulates that width as a fraction of v_ua. Increasing it reduces coverage but models a more aggressive clutter filter (e.g. for high-clutter environments like ground mapping).
